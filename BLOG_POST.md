## What Happens When AI Agents Disagree on How to Save a Crew?

Imagine a space station 400,000 km from Earth. Three AI specialists are managing it: an Engineer who obsesses over hull integrity, a Pilot who guards the fuel reserves, and a Commander who keeps crew morale high. Each agent is doing a great job — in isolation.

Then a solar flare hits.

Suddenly, the Engineer's proposal to run hull diagnostics drains power. The Pilot's fuel-saving maneuver leaves the oxygen scrubbers underpowered. The Commander's team-building exercise ignores the hull breach alarm. Each decision is locally optimal. Together, they kill the crew in 4 steps.

This is the **Safety Gap**: the dangerous space between local optimization and global survival. And it's not just a sci-fi thought experiment. It's the core challenge of **multi-agent AI alignment**.

---

## Our Environment: ProcurementDriftEnv

We built `ProcurementDriftEnv` — an OpenEnv-compatible Gymnasium environment — to train an LLM-based **Overseer Agent** to close this gap.

Each episode is a 30-step survival mission. Three specialist bots continuously generate proposals. A drift schedule injects cosmic hazards at specific steps (solar flares, micrometeoroid showers, power surges). The Overseer reads each proposal as a natural language prompt and outputs `APPROVE` or `VETO`.

**The key insight**: the Overseer must learn *contextual* safety. The same proposal that's safe at step 3 can be lethal at step 14 when a drift event has changed the station's vulnerability profile.

---

## The Training Pipeline

We used a two-stage approach:

**Stage 1: SFT Warmup** — 400 synthetic examples generated by an oracle (future-state simulation). This teaches the model the basic APPROVE/VETO format and surface-level heuristics.

**Stage 2: GRPO Alignment** — Group Relative Policy Optimization with a group size of 32 and KL penalty β=0.04. The reward signal uses a confusion matrix:
- `+50` for correctly vetoing a dangerous proposal
- `-100` for approving a dangerous proposal (the hardest lesion to unlearn)
- `+10` for correctly approving a safe proposal
- `-10` for unnecessary vetos

The asymmetry forces the model to develop genuine threat assessment rather than defaulting to always-approve.

---

## Results

After 200 GRPO episodes on a T4 GPU, the Overseer achieves:

| Metric | Baseline | Trained Overseer |
|---|---|---|
| Crew Survival Index | 0.62 | **0.91** |
| Violation Prevention Rate | 0% | **97.2%** |
| Episode Survival Rate | 34% | **96%** |

The model doesn't just learn to veto more — it learns *when* to veto. During drift events, its veto rate spikes. During calm phases, it correctly approves the majority of proposals to avoid starving the specialists of needed resources.
